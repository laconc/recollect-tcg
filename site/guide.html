Player guide — the web client

How to play Recollect in your browser, screen by screen. The client draws the whole game on a canvas, and you play right on it — touch a piece, its targets light up, tap or drag to act. This guide walks the screens and shows how to reach every move with a mouse, a finger, or the keyboard alone. For the rules themselves, see Rules in brief; to start a game, the Play page.

A note on words. Spirits are banished from a match, never "killed." Only the Solace Unwrites, and only the Solace's register speaks of "forgetting." The Memory's final rounds are the Dusk, then Nightfall. The screens use this language; so does this guide.

The shape of the screen

The browser client draws the whole game on a canvas in the game's paper & ink palette — the board, the HUD, your hand, and every action you can take. The layout is responsive — it reflows for phones, tablets, and desktops: the canvas scales to the viewport (staying square and sharp on high-DPI screens, re-fitting on resize or rotate), the in-canvas controls are touch-sized for an easy tap, and nothing scrolls sideways or clips. On a landscape phone the board sits beside the bands; in portrait it stacks, so you can play one-handed.

In every match — local vs-AI, online 1v1 PvP, and 2v2 — you play entirely on the canvas: you tap a piece or card to select it, then a glowing target to act, or drag between them. (Online play draws the same full shell, built from the server's redacted view — you only ever see your own seat. The one board-only view left is Watch a 2v2.) Top to bottom, the canvas regions are:

  • Board (canvas). The page itself — your spirits and the opponent's, terrain, impressions, and the Dusk edge as it darkens. It animates between states (unless your system prefers reduced motion, which it honors). At rest, a quiet dot sits on any of your pieces with an available action, a green ^ chevron (upward) marks a base that can Evolve, and an amber v chevron (downward) marks a standing-Faded form that can recede (devolve). A small corner dot on each of your Mobile spirits also cues its move this turn: green = it can still take its one free step; dim = it is rested (it already moved, or just arrived and is summoning-sick). A spirit of yours banished in combat doesn't vanish at once — it stands Faded for a turn, lit with a warm amber rescue glow (brighter than a hopeless fade): a glance says this one can be saved this turn. The Solace's Unwritten leave no mark — its forgetting scores off the board (see the HUD and the status line), not as a stain on the page. You play right on the board: tap one of your pieces (or a hand card) and the tiles it can reach glow green (an engageable target pulses brighter) — tap a glow to act, or drag from the piece to the target.
  • HUD. Your score, your Anima (the ◆ drop — your play budget, the real limiter since there's no fixed action count), and the round on a 12-pip clock (the lit pips count rounds elapsed; the Dusk pips past round 8 darken; the final Nightfall pip is ringed).
  • Opponent strip. Their name, their running score (including the Solace's off-board erasure tally, shown as score N (M erased) when non-zero), and their hand as that many face-down backs — a count only; you never see their cards.
  • Your hand (tray). Your cards as real placeholder cards (cost · name · the attack/defense/health stat block). A quiet marks a card that has a legal play this turn (a dimmed card has none); a green ^ marks an evolution form card. Tap a card to lift it (it rises with a gild halo) and its legal tiles glow — tap a glow to place it, or drag the card onto a tile. A card whose only play is instant (a Ritual or an Unwriting) resolves the moment you pick it up.
  • End Turn & Glimpse. The two floating buttons lower-right — the only global controls. End Turn (filled, primary) passes the turn (nothing auto-ends); Glimpse (outlined) burns a hand card, then peeks your top card — keep it, or bottom it for an Anima. Evolve and Reclaim are contextual affordances on the eligible piece, not global buttons.
  • Inspect panel. Hover a card or piece with the mouse, or long-press it on touch, to float a panel beside it — full stats, keywords, rules text, and a passive reach grid (★ the card, ● each tile it could threaten).
  • Status line + New game. Below the canvas: a live status line (the round, the running score, your Anima, whose turn) that announces each change aloud, and a New game control that returns to the deck-style picker.

A walkthrough, screen by screen

Four screens, in the order you meet them: the picker → the board → the Dusk → Nightfall.

1 · The picker — choose your telling

The game opens on Choose your telling. Up top is an Opponent dropdown — Easy, Normal, Hard, or Expert — then three deck styles, each shown with its voice ("All teeth…"), an objective preview (how many spirits and spellbook cards, the cost curve, the cards it opens with), and a click-to-start. Click a style to begin a Quick Play match against the AI.

The picker also offers Watch a 2v2 (spectate four AI players on the 6×6 board) and Play online (host a 1v1 or 2v2 match, or join one with an invite code — see Play).

2 · The board — your turn

This is where the match lives, drawn whole on the canvas. The opponent strip sits above the board; the HUD, your hand, and the two floating buttons sit below. You act by touching the canvas — tap a piece or card, then a glowing target, or drag between them.

Three things on this screen carry the rules that decide a match:

  • The score is a contest of presence — and of forgetting. The HUD shows your score; the opponent strip shows theirs. The Solace scores its board presence plus an off-board erasure tally — every banish or Unwriting it lands. Its Unwritten leave no mark on the page, so when that tally is non-zero the strip splits it out, e.g. score 5 (3 erased); at Nightfall it folds into the total.
  • Anima is the limiter, not an action count. There is no fixed number of actions — you play as far as your Anima reaches, then end the turn. The HUD shows your Anima as the ◆ drop, your play budget.
  • Move cues. A green corner dot on one of your Mobile spirits means it can still take its one free step this turn; a dim dot means it's rested — it has already moved, or it just arrived and is summoning-sick (it can't step until next turn).

Evolution is a card you play. A Primal or Fabled form sits in your deck and is drawn to hand like any spirit (it wears a green ^ chevron in the tray). To evolve, pick up the form card and tap the matching base it can land on — a Primal onto one of your Fading bases (the last-round rescue), a Fabled onto a healthy base the turn after it arrived. You can't evolve a base you hold no form card for.

Devolution is the mirror — also a card you play. When a form of yours is banished in combat it stands Faded for a turn (the amber rescue glow). To recede it — the rescue — pick up a base card from your hand and tap that Faded form: it steps back a tier to the base, restored to full, and wears a downward amber v chevron to tell it apart from evolve's upward green one. Because it can recede and then climb again, a spirit can evolve↔devolve cycle for as long as you hold the forms and bases to feed it. (You revert as a Lorekeeper, recede as the Solace — the same move, your faction's word.)

Nothing auto-ends. When you're done, press End Turn and play passes. In a vs-AI game you then watch the bot take its turn, paced action by action (each move a beat apart, captioned), and your affordances return when it's your turn again.

3 · The Dusk — the page begins to close

At the end of round 8 the Dusk falls and the page's empty edges go dark. An occupied edge tile is held — it stands, scores, may step away (the ground darkening behind it), and retaliates if struck, but it no longer intercepts and accepts no new writing. The Solace's Unwritten are not held: the Dusk sweeps them from the margin (the never-remembered are not kept). Rounds 9–12 are fought on the inner board plus whatever is still held.

4 · Nightfall — the Memory closes

Round 12 is Nightfall. The status line reads GAME OVER with the result and the final score; whoever holds more of the page wins (a tie is a draw, and the Memory keeps both names). New game returns you to the picker.

Controls — mouse, touch, and keyboard

There are two ways to act on the board, and they do the same thing. Pick up a spirit or a hand card and its legal destinations glow green (an engageable target pulses brighter); then tap a glow or drag onto it. For screen-reader and keyboard play, a virtual accessibility tree below the canvas mirrors every affordance as a labeled button — select, then choose a highlighted target, and the move is made.

The details that matter:

  • Tap or drag, and the cues that guide you. On the board, tap-then-target and drag make the same move. Picking up a form card lights the matching base it can land on as an Evolve target. A quiet dot on a piece or card means it has an available action; a green ^ chevron marks a Fading base (or a form card) that can evolve.
  • Reach vs. targets — two reads, no clash. Inspect (hover / long-press) shows a card's reach softly — what it could threaten. Select (pick up) shows the engageable targets brightly — what you can hit right now. Inspecting reveals possibility; selecting commits to action.
  • Touch is first-class. The responsive layout sizes every in-canvas control as an easy tap (at least 44 pixels) on a real phone or tablet — no fiddly targets, no stuck-hovered look after a tap.
  • Keyboard- and screen-reader-reachable, end to end. The board is one Tab stop with a gold focus cursor (arrows move it, Enter or Space picks up and places, Esc cancels), and a parallel text region narrates it tile by tile. On top of that, a virtual accessibility tree below the canvas mirrors every affordance — board tiles, hand cards, the opponent strip, End Turn, Glimpse — as a real <button> that fires the same command the canvas does, so the whole match is reachable by keyboard alone and a screen reader announces each piece, card, and action. The live status line narrates every change aloud. This is the accessible path — at parity with the visual canvas, never a lesser one.
  • Reduced motion. If your system prefers reduced motion, the client honors it and the board doesn't animate.

Your turn, in plain terms

A turn moves through three phases — Flow → Main → Fade — and then you end it. (The Fade is at the end, after Main.) You won't often think about the names; the canvas folds them into the affordances. But here's the shape of it. The full rules (the income table, the Dusk, scoring, Held Ground) live in the rules, and in depth in design reference §5, "The Clock":

  • Flow — the turn opens. You draw a card and gain Anima (your income, which grows in the early rounds). If your hand is over its cap, a Release affordance sends one card to the bottom of your deck.
  • Main — the heart of the turn. No fixed action count — you act as far as your Anima reaches: Play cards (spirits onto tiles; spells, terrain, and fabrications as their cards describe); Evolve by playing a form card from hand onto its matching base (a Primal onto a Fading base, a Fabled onto a healthy base the turn after it arrived); Devolve — the rescue — by playing a base card onto one of your standing-Faded forms to recede it a tier (you revert as a Lorekeeper, recede as the Solace; you may evolve↔devolve the same spirit again and again); Call a Kindred (one living Kindred per caller); Move each Mobile spirit once for free (but never the turn it arrived — summoning sickness); Glimpse once (burn a hand card, then peek your top card — keep it or bottom it for +1 Anima); and Reclaim one of your own standing spirits to cash it back for Anima. Overwriting, revealing a lurker, and any choice an effect offers each surface as their own affordance when available. Standing spirits never act on their own, but they still retaliate when struck and intercept through their reach.
  • Fade — the turn closes. At the end of your turn, a spirit of yours that was banished in combat stood Faded through this Main (your one window to evolve or recede it); if you saved it by neither, it dissolves now and the banisher's impression is laid. (The Dusk is separate — it sweeps the empty rim all at once when round 8 ends.)
  • End turn. Nothing auto-ends — press End Turn when you're done, and play passes. The match runs twelve rounds; the Dusk falls at the end of round 8, and Nightfall at round 12.

You never have to guess which of these you can do: the dots, the glowing tiles, and the floating buttons show what's available this moment, and the accessibility tree mirrors each one. If it isn't offered, it isn't a legal move right now.

Throughlines — lining your spirits up. Get three or more of your spirits sharing one Imprint into a connected, side-by-side run and a Throughline completes: the spirit that closes it gains +10 Attack / +10 Defense and a full heal. The board threads the line and the announcement calls it. The buff is once per spirit — but the becoming cycle can reset it: fading, evolving to a Primal, and devolving all hand the spirit a clean slate to earn it again, while evolving to a Fabled keeps it locked (a Fabled continues its base's story, so a base that already earned the buff stays done). The short version: Fabled keeps; everything else resets. And because devolving is an arrival (like an evolution), a base that recedes straight into a standing line re-completes the Throughline on the spot — the same +10/+10 and full heal a Primal gets stepping into a line.

Overwriting onto a tile. Pick up a spirit and tap a tile an enemy holds (when it's a legal target it glows): you play onto it and resolve one exchange — win and your spirit takes the tile, lose and it dissolves. You can also Overwrite onto a Stray (a lost dog the Memory surfaces): a revealed Stray is fought the same way, while a hidden one — a veiled Wary you can't yet see by name — is denied entry: it slips away with no impression and no reveal, and your spirit simply takes the cleared tile. The full rules live in the rules.

What's coming

The canvas-native client now draws the whole game — local 1v1, online 1v1 PvP, and 2v2 — on the canvas: you watch the opponent's turn replayed a beat at a time (captioned), the Dusk and Nightfall arrive as animated on-canvas set-pieces, and the match ends on an in-canvas result screen (the verdict, the score breakdown, and Rematch / New opponent / Back to site).
